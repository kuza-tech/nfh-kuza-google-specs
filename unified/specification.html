1. Executive Summary

NFH-Kuza-Google is a unified service-orchestration platform that connects seekers (individuals, households, patients) with providers (agripreneurs, vets, aggregators, health facilities, clinicians) through a single flow engine delivered over WhatsApp and web apps, using the Beckn open network protocol for interoperability.

The platform now serves two domains in parallel, sharing the same architecture, channels, flow engine, and data infrastructure:

DomainBeckn CodePrimary PartnersFlows
Local Services Agri / Rural livelihoods local-services Kuza, agripreneur networks, rural aggregators Livelihood, Household, Market, Advisory
Health Services TB / National Disease Programme health-services National Disease Programme, Ministry of Health (Kenya), Global Fund, county health systems TB Screening, TB Treatment, TB Supervision, Post-TB Lung Health

Partners

Kuza — Platform technology, seeker & provider networks, field operations, domain expertise (agri + health).
Google — Cloud infrastructure (GCP) hosting the unified stack; Content APIs serving curated domain content (agri, health, market, finance); AI interfaces (location normalisation, content tailoring, triage assist, translation, generation).
Beckn — Open network protocol, DeDi Registry, ONIX adapters, Catalog & Discovery Services — one network, two domain codes.
National Disease Programme — Kenya National TB & Leprosy Programme — health-domain partner, clinical guidelines, disease-programme register alignment, Global Fund compliance.

2. Unified Flow Engine — 7-Step Model

All interactions on the platform — across both domains — are expressed as instances of a single 7-step flow. A "flow" is any journey where a seeker with a need is matched to a provider who can serve it and whose outcome is recorded. Whether the need is "vaccinate 200 birds" or "screen me for TB", the pipeline is the same.

Why this matters

A single conversational engine, a single order state machine, a single analytics pipeline, and a single Beckn adapter serve both domains. Adding a new vertical (HIV, malaria, cash-transfer) becomes a schema + taxonomy exercise — not a new platform.

2.3 Flow Initiation Modes

Not every flow starts with a seeker asking the chatbot. The engine supports three initiation modes — same 7 steps, different trigger:

ModeWho triggersExample
SEEKER_INITIATED Seeker via WhatsApp Farmer requests vaccination; patient with cough asks for screening
PROVIDER_INITIATED Provider / Coordinator proactively (batch or single) CHP conducts Active Case Finding in a village (see §5.9); extension officer runs mass vaccination campaign
SYSTEM_INITIATED Flow Engine itself — triggered by an outcome event, scheduled policy, or cascade rule Contact tracing: positive TB diagnosis auto-creates screening flows for household/workplace/school contacts (see §5.10); outbreak quarantine triggers vaccination flows for neighbouring farms

2.4 Cascading & Linked Flows

A flow's Outcome step can become the Need step of one or more new flows. Every flow records:

  • triggerFlowId — reference to the parent flow that spawned this one (absent for root flows)
  • triggerReason — CONTACT_OF_POSITIVE, OUTBREAK_QUARANTINE, SCHEDULED_FOLLOWUP, ROUTINE_RECALL, etc.
  • indexEntityId (vertical-specific) — the seeker whose outcome initiated the cascade (index patient UUID for TB contact tracing)

The parent/child linkage is preserved in the data layer, enabling surveillance (what % of contacts got screened?), yield analysis (how many new positives per index case?), and longitudinal outcome tracking.

2.5 Batch / Campaign Flows

One provider session can produce many flows. A CHP visits a village, screens 40 people — one session, 40 flows, sharing a batchId. The batch is a grouping for logistics, stipend payment, and reporting — each individual flow still has its own seeker, its own Record step, and its own Outcome.

  • batchId (UUID) — groups flows into a session
  • batchType — CAMPAIGN, COMMUNITY_VISIT, CONTACT_TRACING_SESSION, OUTBREAK_RESPONSE

Why batches matter: CHPs claim stipends per session, supervisors review batch completeness, analytics measure per-session yield.

3. Actors — Generalised Roles

Rather than hard-coding "Farmer" and "Provider", the platform models five abstract roles. A single individual can occupy different roles in different flows.

3.1 Seeker

An individual or household with a need that can be served by the network. A seeker may be active (they start the flow themselves) or passive (the flow is started on their behalf by a provider or by the system).

  • Farmer / Rural Household Local — discovers and books rural services; sells produce. Usually active.
  • Patient / Family Health — discovers TB screening, treatment, post-TB follow-up. Active when self-presenting.
  • Household contact Health — needs preventive screening after a household TB case. Usually passive — the flow is triggered by the index patient's positive outcome (see §5.10 Contact Tracing).
  • Community member under ACF outreach Health — screened during a CHP community visit. Passive — CHP initiates the flow, seeker consents to participate (see §5.9 ACF).
  • Primary interface: WhatsApp for active seekers; outbound WhatsApp / SMS notification for passive seekers (informing them of a scheduled visit or requested consent).
  • Profile captured once: name, phone, location (village/ward/county), optional GPS, preferred language, domain-specific attributes (enterprise for farmer; gender/DOB/consent for patient).

3.2 Facilitator Local Health

An intermediary — typically a community-level worker — who initiates flows on behalf of seekers, captures consent, performs the initial service interaction (symptom screening, input-need assessment), and bridges seekers to providers. The Facilitator is the "last mile" of service delivery: they operate in the community (not in a facility or shop) and know the households they serve personally.

  • CHP / CHV Health — Community Health Promoter / Volunteer. Conducts door-to-door symptom screening, contact tracing, post-TB follow-up visits. Refers suspected cases to facilities for diagnostic testing.
  • Agent / Agripreneur (in facilitation role) Local — rural agents and field-based agripreneurs who help farmers identify needs, discover the right provider, and complete transactions. Also deliver training and advisory at the household.
  • Extension Agent (field role) Local — field officers conducting on-site visits (distinct from the Coordinator role which is supervisory).
  • Primary interface: WhatsApp for quick actions + Web App (opened from WhatsApp link) for catalog / history / batch management.
  • Typical flow role: initiates PROVIDER_INITIATED flows on behalf of seekers (e.g. CHP conducting village screening); handles SYSTEM_INITIATED cascade flows (e.g. contact-tracing visits assigned by the engine).
Facilitator vs. Provider — why the distinction matters. A Facilitator enables the service; the Provider delivers the definitive service. A CHP does the initial symptom screening but the facility confirms TB. An agent helps a farmer find a vet but the vet administers vaccination. The distinction shapes provider-matching in discover, payment flows (stipends for facilitators vs fees for providers), and accountability for clinical outcomes.

3.3 Provider

An entity that delivers the definitive service, product, or content. Providers are typically located at a fixed address (facility, shop, warehouse) or provide the final professional / clinical output even if mobile.

  • Service providers Local — vets, agro-vets, mechanisation operators, tradespeople who deliver the final service.
  • Input / Goods suppliers Local — feed stores, seed suppliers, household goods shops.
  • Buyers / Aggregators Local — produce buyers in the Market-Produce flow (inverted economics).
  • Content providers Local Health — advisory content sources (e.g. Google Content APIs per domain).
  • Health Facilities Health — dispensaries, health centres, sub-county and county referral hospitals, labs (GeneXpert, culture, X-ray).
  • Clinical staff Health — clinicians, nurses, lab technicians, pharmacy staff (linked to a facility).
  • Primary interface: WhatsApp notifications + browser-based Web App for detailed management.
  • Agripreneurs sometimes play the Provider role (as direct seller / aggregator) and sometimes the Facilitator role (introducing farmer to another provider). Context determines which applies.

3.4 Coordinator

Supervisory or oversight roles that operate across many Facilitators and Providers.

  • Area / County-level agri coordinators Local — supervise agents, extension officers, field teams.
  • SCTLCs Health — Sub-County TB & Leprosy Coordinators.
  • CTLCs Health — County TB & Leprosy Coordinators.
  • National Disease Programme Director Health — national programme leadership with oversight across all counties.
  • In the platform, Coordinators appear as Seekers when consuming supervision flows, and as Validators when signing off on visits.

3.5 Validator

Labs, auditors, QA reviewers — parties whose confirmation is required to close a flow.

  • Labs Health — return GeneXpert / culture / DST results.
  • Data-quality auditors Health — validate facility data quality and mortality audits.
  • Agri-input auditors Local — optional QA on input provenance.

3.6 Sponsor

Entities that fund, commission, or benefit from aggregate outcomes.

  • Ministry of Health, Global Fund, TaifaCare Health
  • Donors, NGOs, government programmes Local Health

5.5 TB Screening Flow — CHP-Facilitated (Primary Path) Health

In health, screening is primarily done by CHPs — not by patients self-presenting. The CHP is the Facilitator who goes to households and communities, conducts symptom-based screening, captures consent, and refers suspected cases to the Provider (facility / lab / clinician) for definitive diagnostic tests.
  • Initiation mode: PROVIDER_INITIATED (by Facilitator)
  • Seeker = household / patient (passive — did not request the visit)
  • Facilitator = CHP / CHV (conducts visit, symptom screening, referral)
  • Provider = Health Facility (GeneXpert, clinician confirms diagnosis)
Other entry paths into the same screening lifecycle are documented separately:
  • §5.5b Patient Self-Presentation — SEEKER_INITIATED variant (less common in rural contexts)
  • §5.9 Active Case Finding (campaign-style) — same facilitator pattern but planned as a multi-day batch event (batchType: CAMPAIGN)
  • §5.10 Contact Tracing — SYSTEM_INITIATED cascades from a positive-outcome index case, often assigned to CHPs for household visits

Example: CHP Grace is assigned to Katolo village in Nyakach Sub-County, Kisumu County. She does routine household-by-household screening. At one house, Joseph mentions a 3-week cough.

Figure 5.5 — CHP-facilitated TB screening. The Seeker (patient/household) is a passive participant throughout the Facilitator row; the Provider (facility / lab) takes over for the diagnostic Deliver+Record stage.

CHP-Facilitated Flow (Step by Step)

  1. CHP Grace opens the CHP app (launched from a WhatsApp link). Today's village-visit list loads.
  2. Grace taps [Start Visit] for Katolo village. Flow Engine creates a batch: batchType: COMMUNITY_VISIT, batchId: b4f1c8d6-katolo-2026-04-14.
  3. Grace arrives at Joseph's household. Introduces purpose, captures Joseph's consent (Seeker role activated — passively, he agrees to participate).
  4. Consent recorded with timestamp. A new individual flow is created for Joseph, initiationMode: PROVIDER_INITIATED, linked to the batch.
  5. Grace runs structured symptom screening in the app: cough > 2 weeks? fever? weight loss? night sweats? TB contact history?
  6. Joseph: "Yes, I've been coughing for 3 weeks."
  7. App scores symptoms. Decision: presumptive TB.
  8. Branch:
    • If not presumptive → Grace records screening, gives health advice, flow moves to OUTCOME_RECORDED with outcomeType: SCREENING_NEGATIVE → CLOSED.
    • If presumptive → continue.
  9. App calls discover for nearby facilities with capability GeneXpert. Grace picks Kisumu County Hospital in the app.
  10. Referral generated: slip ID REF-KATOLO-0042. Facility notified via WhatsApp (queued in their Web App). Joseph notified: "Kindly visit Kisumu County Hospital tomorrow with this slip. Grace will assist you."
  11. Sputum sample collected (in-home if CHP is trained, or at a nearby collection point). Sample transported to lab.
  12. Facility / Lab receives the sample. Provider role now active.
  13. Lab runs GeneXpert; result returned.
  14. Clinician records result in the facility Web App — writes back to the disease-programme register via Joseph's patient UUID.
  15. Both Grace (CHP) and Joseph notified via WhatsApp: "Test result is ready. Please come to the clinic to discuss." (clinical result is delivered by a clinician in person, not by the bot.)
  16. Outcome:
    • POSITIVE → system cascades TWO flows: a TB Treatment flow for Joseph (SYSTEM_INITIATED, triggerReason: OUTCOME_FOLLOW_ON) and a batch of Contact Tracing screening flows for Joseph's identified contacts (§5.10).
    • NEGATIVE → follow-up advice; flow CLOSED.
    • LOST_TO_FOLLOWUP → patient or sample didn't arrive within 7 days; Grace receives a follow-up task.

5.5b Alternative Path — Patient Self-Presentation (SEEKER_INITIATED)

A minority of patients who know about TB may approach the chatbot directly without waiting for a CHP visit. The lifecycle is identical from step Provider Side onward (same referral, sample collection, lab processing, outcome handling, cascade). The variant affects only the initial Facilitator stage:

  • Step 1 replaces CHP visit: Patient sends "Hi" via WhatsApp → Flow Handler creates a flow with initiationMode: SEEKER_INITIATED.
  • Steps 2–7 (symptom screening and consent): conducted via WhatsApp chat instead of a CHP visit. The symptom questions are the same; the app scores presumption the same way.
  • Step 8b (referral): the patient picks a facility from a list returned by discover.
  • Steps 9–16: identical to the primary path. Sample collection, lab, recording, outcome, and cascade behaviour are unchanged.

For analytics, the two paths are distinguishable by initiationMode and by the absence of a batchId in self-presentation flows. In practice, self-presentation is rare in rural settings — the overwhelming majority of cases enter via CHP-facilitated screening or contact tracing.

5.6 TB Treatment & Adherence Flow Health

Initiation mode: usually SYSTEM_INITIATED — auto-cascaded from the TB_POSITIVE outcome of a screening flow (§5.5 self-presentation, §5.9 CHP outreach, or §5.10 contact tracing). Occasionally SEEKER_INITIATED when a patient transfers in with an external diagnosis. Either way, triggerFlowId is set to the screening flow when available.

Figure 5.6 — TB Treatment: Monthly cycle repeats for the full regimen; outcome determines next-step flow.

Flow Summary

  • Need: Patient enrolled in treatment (usually auto-linked from TB Screening positive outcome).
  • Search / Select / Confirm: Monthly visit scheduled at the treatment facility; patient confirms via WhatsApp.
  • Deliver: Drug dispensing at pharmacy; sputum conversion check at 2 months; daily adherence reminder via WhatsApp template.
  • Record: Each monthly visit generates a record in disease-programme registers (e.g., DSTB / DRTB / TPT for TB) via patient UUID. ADRs captured through a structured WhatsApp form.
  • Outcome: At end of regimen — CURED, COMPLETED, FAILED, DIED, LOST_TO_FOLLOWUP. CURED auto-creates a Post-TB Lung Health flow at the 6-month follow-up mark.

5.7 TB Supervision Flow Health

Initiation mode: PROVIDER_INITIATED (coordinator-initiated) — the SCTLC / CTLC opens their dashboard and starts a supervision cycle. Can also be SYSTEM_INITIATED when a facility's quality indicators cross a threshold and the engine auto-schedules a supervision visit (triggerReason: QUALITY_INDICATOR_BREACH). A quarterly supervision round typically creates a COMMUNITY_VISIT-style batch covering all facilities in the coordinator's catchment.

Figure 5.7 — TB Supervision: the same 7-step engine with Coordinator as Seeker and Facility as Provider.

5.8 Post-TB Lung Health Flow Health

Initiation mode: SYSTEM_INITIATED — cascaded automatically from a TB treatment flow whose outcome is CURED or TREATMENT_COMPLETED. triggerFlowId points back to the treatment flow, triggerReason: SCHEDULED_FOLLOWUP. The patient is notified via WhatsApp and invited to the first post-TB visit.

5.9 Active Case Finding — Campaign-Style Mass Screening Health

Relationship to §5.5: routine CHP-facilitated screening (ongoing, household-by-household) is covered in §5.5. This section describes the campaign variant — a planned, time-limited, often multi-day mass screening event involving many Facilitators working from a common plan. Shares the same Facilitator/Provider mechanics as 5.5, but is organised as a large batchType: CAMPAIGN with additional logistics.

When ACF campaigns run: WHO-driven screening weeks, county response to a TB hotspot, Ministry of Health push, funding-partner-led programmes (e.g. Global Fund grant-funded mass ACF). Typically covers a sub-county or county, involves dozens of CHPs, is planned by a Coordinator (SCTLC/CTLC), and runs for days to weeks.

Why ACF campaigns matter: WHO estimates roughly one third of TB cases go undiagnosed each year because symptomatic patients never present to a facility. Routine CHP screening catches incremental cases. Campaigns catch many cases quickly in hotspot areas — evidence from Kenya's National TB Programme shows campaign ACF finds more than twice as many TB cases per CHP-hour compared with passive screening.

5.9.1 Example Scenario — County-Wide Campaign

Kisumu CTLC launches a 2-week campaign covering 6 sub-counties after a surveillance signal shows rising TB notifications. 40 CHPs are activated. Each CHP conducts the same CHP-Facilitated screening flow described in §5.5, but every flow is tagged with the campaign batch.

  1. Shwetha opens the CHP app (launched from a WhatsApp link). App pulls her assigned list for today.
  2. The CTLC creates a campaign batch: batchType: CAMPAIGN, batchId: camp-kisumu-april-2026-acf, validity 2 weeks, target: 6 sub-counties.
  3. 40 CHPs are enrolled in the campaign. Each CHP's daily screening sessions now roll up under both: their own per-day COMMUNITY_VISIT sub-batch AND the campaign-wide CAMPAIGN batch.
  4. During the campaign, each CHP conducts the same household-visit screening flow described in §5.5 (primary path). Every flow created carries batchId: camp-kisumu-april-2026-acf.
  5. Positive/presumptive screenings are referred to facilities identically to the routine flow (see §5.5 steps 8b-11).
  6. End-of-campaign dashboards aggregate over 40 CHPs × 10 days: total screened, total referred, conversion rates, hotspot sub-counties, yield per CHP.
  7. After campaign closes, CTLC reviews results; may trigger follow-on surveillance or re-plan based on findings.

5.9.3 Operational Value (vs routine §5.5)

  • Scale: campaigns compress many weeks of routine screening into days, effective for hotspot response or programme-wide scaling.
  • Funding: campaigns are often funded by a sponsor (Ministry, Global Fund); the campaign batchId anchors donor reporting and claims.
  • Supervision: heavier coordinator involvement — CTLC runs the campaign dashboard, daily progress checks, CHP support.
  • Analytics: campaign yield benchmarks (positive rate, cost-per-case-found) inform whether to repeat, target differently, or transition back to routine screening.

5.10 Contact Tracing — System-Initiated Cascade Health

Tuberculosis is airborne and communicable. When a patient tests positive, everyone in prolonged close contact with them during the infectious period must also be screened. The Flow Engine automates this: a positive outcome on one flow triggers a fan-out of new screening flows, one per identified contact, each linked via triggerFlowId and marked initiationMode = SYSTEM_INITIATED.

5.10.1 Contact Identification

During the index patient's interview (part of RECORD step), the clinician / CHP enumerates contacts:

  • Household contacts — everyone living in the same residence
  • Workplace colleagues — for patients in enclosed workplaces (offices, factories, transport)
  • School classmates and teachers — for student or teacher patients
  • Social / close friends — individuals reported as spending significant time with the patient
  • Neighbours — for densely populated informal settlements

5.10.2 Cascade Mechanism

Index Flow F1 (SEEKER_INITIATED or PROVIDER_INITIATED)
    Patient Mary presents with cough. Screened, tested, diagnosed.
    outcomeType: TB_POSITIVE

Flow Engine rule fires: "positive TB diagnosis triggers contact tracing"

For each contact identified during F1.RECORD step:
    New Flow Fi created with:
        initiationMode: SYSTEM_INITIATED
        triggerFlowId: F1
        triggerReason: CONTACT_OF_POSITIVE
        batchId: (shared across all contacts of this index case)
        batchType: CONTACT_TRACING_SESSION
        verticalAttributes.vertical: health-tb-contact-tracing
        verticalAttributes.contactRelationship: HOUSEHOLD | COWORKER | CLASSMATE | ...
        verticalAttributes.indexPatientUuid: (Mary's UUID)
        verticalAttributes.exposureWindow: { startDate, endDate }

Each Fi then progresses independently through:
    Need (auto-registered) → Search → Select (CHP home visit) → Confirm → Deliver → Record → Outcome

5.10.3 Outbound Notifications

Contact-traced individuals are passive participants — they didn't start the flow. The Flow Engine proactively reaches them via WhatsApp (or SMS if WhatsApp unavailable):

Example WhatsApp notification to contact: 
Habari. You have been identified as a contact of someone recently diagnosed with TB. Don't worry — TB is treatable, and early screening protects you and your family.

A Community Health Promoter will visit your home on [date] at [time] for a free screening. Reply [YES] to confirm, or [RESCHEDULE] if the time does not work.

This is a private, confidential communication. We do not share the name of the index patient.

The notification is non-identifying (never names the index patient) and respects consent. If the contact declines or cannot be reached, the flow transitions to LOST_TO_FOLLOWUP for surveillance reporting.

5.10.4 Clinical and Ethical Safeguards

  • Consent required: index patient must consent to share contacts; each contact must consent to screening. Both recorded with timestamps.
  • No identifying disclosure: index patient's identity is never revealed to contacts.
  • Data minimisation: only contact-tracing-relevant data flows to the cascaded flows (exposure window, relationship type, geographic overlap) — not the index patient's full clinical record.
  • Stigma mitigation: outbound messaging is framed positively (treatable, free, protective) per WHO guidance.

5.10.5 Analytics Value

Linkage (triggerFlowId chain) enables surveillance metrics that are otherwise impossible:

  • Contact-investigation completeness: what % of identified contacts of a positive case are actually screened within 2 weeks?
  • Yield: how many new cases are found per index case, by relationship type (household vs workplace vs school)?
  • Cluster detection: are positive cases clustering around specific index patients, locations, or time windows?
  • Longitudinal impact: post-TB outcomes for the cohort of index+contacts over months / years.

8. Domain Detection & User Routing

Both domains share a single WhatsApp entry point but route to domain-specific handlers. The platform determines which domain a seeker belongs to and which menu to show.

Figure 8.1 — A single webhook; a lightweight router dispatches to the correct domain handler.

Routing Strategy

  1. Role lookup: If the WhatsApp wa_id is already registered with a domain, route directly to that handler. A user can be registered with more than one domain; in that case, the last-used domain is default.
  2. Trigger words: First-time or ambiguous messages can carry a trigger — "screen me", "cough", "TB" → health; "vaccine", "eggs", "school fees" → local.
  3. Menu-driven disambiguation: If role and triggers are absent, the system shows a top-level domain menu: "Are you looking for: (a) Rural services (b) Health services".
  4. Cross-domain re-entry: A user can switch domains mid-conversation by typing "menu" or via a persistent footer button.

Example — Two Users, Two Domains

Farmer Mary sends "Hi" → router finds role=FARMER → Local Services Domain Flow Handler → "Welcome. What do you need help with?" [Livelihood] [Household] [Market] [Advisory]

Patient Joseph sends "Hi" → router finds role=PATIENT → Health Domain Flow Handler → "Welcome. How can we help?" [Get Screened] [Treatment Help] [Find Facility] [Health Info]

10. Location & Matching

Location Capture

  • Seeker provides location as free text (village/ward/county) or GPS live location.
  • Google AI normalises free text to structured County + Sub-County.
  • System confirms normalised location with seeker before proceeding.

Provider / Facility Matching

  • Providers register explicit service areas (County + Sub-County combinations).
  • Health facilities additionally have capability filters (e.g., GeneXpert, Spirometry, DRTB-certified).
  • Match logic: seeker's county + sub_county must exist in provider's registered service areas AND the provider must advertise the required capability.

Kenya Geography

17. Appendix — Phased Analytics Roadmap

Roadmap, Not Architecture

This is a roadmap framework, not an architectural component. Analytics is implemented as a logical capability that consumes data from the platform — it is not a separate layer of the stack. The architecture has 6 layers plus external integrations; analytics sits on top of them as a consumer.

Analytics maturity progresses through three phases regardless of domain. The same phases apply to local services, health services, and any future vertical added to the platform.

17.1 Phase 1 — Analytics on Existing Data (Months 1–6)

Use what you already have. No new data collection required — dashboards and analyses sit on top of the operational data captured by the flow engine.

DomainIllustrative Dashboards & Analyses
Local Provider performance dashboards, conversion funnels (discover → select → confirm → deliver), seasonal demand patterns, geographic service-availability heatmaps, cross-category provider utilisation.
Health Facility performance (presumptive-to-confirmed ratio), screening cascade analysis, supervision-visit coverage gaps, financial anomaly detection on payment claims, CHP productivity.

17.2 Phase 2 — Predictive ML (Months 6–18)

Train models on accumulated outcome data once enough longitudinal history has accrued.

DomainIllustrative Models
Local Demand forecasting, provider-matching optimisation, seeker churn prediction, price-signal detection.
Health Patient risk stratification, treatment failure prediction, contact-investigation prioritisation, mortality prevention (NLP on death audits), ADR early warning.

17.3 Phase 3 — Pioneering Surveillance (Months 12–36)

Longitudinal, multi-domain, novel intelligence — the phase that produces datasets and insights not available elsewhere.

  • Local Cross-domain network effects — seekers active in multiple verticals show materially different retention and outcome profiles.
  • Local Multi-vertical seeker journeys — unified view across all verticals a seeker participates in.
  • Health Longitudinal post-TB lung disease surveillance — national-scale dataset on PTLD trajectories.
  • Health Multi-disease platform extension — HIV, malaria, cholera on the same flow engine and taxonomy.

Applies to Any Domain

The three-phase progression — use what you have → predict → pioneer — is domain-agnostic. Every vertical added to the platform (health, agri, finance, education, etc.) inherits the same roadmap. The examples above are illustrative, not prescriptive.
